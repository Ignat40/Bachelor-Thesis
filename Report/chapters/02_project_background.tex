\section{Project Background}
\label{sec:project-background}

Other commercial solutions exist in the market \cite{cboard}, although they are basic, unsupported by their creators, or out of date. The current state of these applications is either hard for children to understand or lacks enough features to have any positive impact on speech. Despite their limitations, they are still currently in use with a number of practicing Speech-Language Pathologists (SLPs), since no other options are available.

Many therapists assign home exercises for children to be done under parental supervision. Unfortunately, parents often do not have enough time or the necessary skills to supervise their children as they do their homework. This can additionally turn the parent-child relationship into a therapist-patient relationship.

In this project, the team has developed and tested a prototype designed to aid children with diagnosed speech impediments. The aim of this prototype is to evaluate whether home exercise participation and technique can be improved through gamification and limited external involvement by parents and SLPs.

\subsubsection*{Key Deliverables}

\begin{itemize}
    \item A prototype that provides guided interactive speech practice exercises.
    \item A web-based dashboard that allows SLPs to create, assign, and manage exercises for patients.
    \item A database-backed system for storing user information, assigned exercises, completed tasks, and progress data.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Expected Outcomes}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Improved motivation in children with speech impediments to perform their home exercises.
    \item Creating a tool that allows SLPs to monitor their patients' activity and progress, so that therapeutic approaches can be adjusted accordingly.
\end{itemize}

\section{Project Scope}
\label{sec:project-scope}

The scope of this project is the development and evaluation of a prototype to test the feasibility of the presented concept. The project experiments with gamification in the design of children's exercises to explore its potential to help children become more self-reliant and consistent in completing prescribed home exercises. During development, the feasibility of future product development is also evaluated. The prototype is based on three exercise types commonly prescribed to children with such impairment.

\subsection{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:problem-statement}

This project identifies and solves three main problems that children with speech impairments, speech therapists, and caregivers commonly face during and outside of clinic practice. Each problem addresses a distinct feature developed in the above-mentioned system. In this section, the abbreviation SLP is used to refer to a speech-language pathologist.

\subsection{Problem 1: Recalling Proper Exercise Execution}

Before addressing any issues, SLPs often begin by conducting articulation exercises with patients \cite{asha_ssd_portal}.  These are meant to strengthen the patient's ability to produce certain tongue and mouth positions. Exercises must be performed with correct form in order to be effective; therefore, patients need a good understanding of proper tongue placement, lip shapes, and breathing patterns to perform them. This can be difficult when patients struggle with certain foundational movements. Even if patients can reproduce the movements correctly during sessions, between sessions, they often forget proper techniques, altering them and reducing the effectiveness of subsequent exercises.

To counter this, parents sometimes try to intervene. Despite their good intentions, this often does more harm than good. Unless parents are trained as SLPs themselves, they are unable to catch errors in patients, and when they do, they are underqualified to accurately correct mistakes in the position drills, leading to improper practice \cite{sugden2020multiple}. These new incorrect patterns have to subsequently be reversed by an SLP before further issues can be addressed. When parents try to step in for SLPs during home practice, unless they are trained in advance, their help is often counterproductive.

Additionally, when parents act as SLPs, this changes the dynamic from parent and child to therapist and patient. This can be harmful to all parties involved; as such, parents are advised to engage in co-play with their kids \cite{klatte2020collaboration}.

The way SLPs currently address this issue is by providing patients with a set of images showing correct mouth positions, accompanied by a short written explanation, printed on paper. This leads to a myriad of problems. First and foremost, when materials are provided on paper, they are easily lost. Secondly, when patients are unable to read, they need a caregiver to read it to them.

Our solution, implemented in this project, addresses that problem through a combination of auditory explanations and two-dimensional visualizations displayed to the user at the start of each exercise. Moreover, only the sounds included in the exercise are shown to the user, eliminating unnecessary information. For example, if this week's assigned exercise focuses on the ``S'' sound, the appropriate visualization and audio are presented to the user. This solution provides a richer, interactive, and more accessible form of guidance.

\subsection[Problem 2: Limited Self-Correction]{Problem 2: Limited Self-Correction Without Mirror Feedback}

While performing exercises, patients often struggle to detect errors on their own. This is partly due to a lack of proprioception, meaning the sense of where certain body parts are in space.

SLPs solve this by having patients do exercises in front of a mirror. This added visual feedback helps patients catch any errors they cannot feel, as well as making it easier for them to match their mouth or tongue position to a provided example. This is also endorsed by ASHA's own clinical practice guidelines \cite{asha_ssd_portal}. In a 2020 study, it was shown that using a mirror, children as young as 3 years old are able to notice deviation from a behavioral standard and correct it accordingly \cite{martin2020mirror}. Additionally, there are currently interactive mirrors on the market that are used as tools to aid in speech therapy \cite{briolight2021mirror}. As such, mirrors have proven to be effective, but they are not always readily applicable to home practice because they are not always available to patients.

Our solution addresses this problem by having a simulated mirror during exercises. This is done by using the front-facing camera of the user's mobile device. Additionally, facial tracking was implemented to analyze mouth position and shape. This information is used to aid in automatic evaluation, as well as provide additional information to SLPs.

\subsection{Problem 3: Need for Therapist-Guided Personalized Exercises}

Patients who struggle with the pronunciation of certain sounds may have difficulty producing them in some words or sentences, even if they are able to do so in isolation \cite{asha_ssd_portal}. This is because different sounds have varying cognitive and motor demands, depending on the surrounding context. Correct reproduction of the sound does not necessarily mean that the underlying issue is solved. Additionally, the root causes of these issues vary widely, usually broken down into mechanical and cognitive categories \cite{farquharson2015language}. Mechanical challenges can include oral-motor dysfunctions, such as tongue-tie, a condition in which the strip of connective tissue under the tongue is malformed \cite{melong2024tonguetie}, or dental issues, such as a gap between the front two teeth, which can cause unintentional whistling \cite{digeronimo2021malocclusion}. On the other hand, issues like receptive language delays, executive function issues, and other language processing disorders can cause cognitive issues \cite{farquharson2015language}. Even if two patients present with the same issue, they can require different exercise approaches \cite{farquharson2015language}.

We understand that the SLP's involvement in the exercise creation process is necessary. As such, our solution is to provide them with a set of tools to aid in the exercise creation process. This is done to ensure the created exercises meet the needs of patients and SLPs. Based on our research and information from practicing SLPs, we have created three different types of exercises:

\enlargethispage{2\baselineskip}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Multiple Choice:} The user is presented with a word and must answer a multiple-choice question related to that word. For example: Sound out the word. Does it start with the letter ``M'' or the letter ``N''?
    \item \textbf{Open Question:} The user is presented with a word and must answer a question either in text or with numbers. For example: Sound out the word. How many times is the letter ``M'' used in this word?
    \item \textbf{Connections:} The user is presented with a set of words that can be connected based on a common denominator. For example: Sound out the words. Connect the words that start with the same letter.
\end{itemize}

Automated evaluation of these exercises can greatly speed up the process, but in order to ensure progress is being made, the SLP's involvement is still necessary. This is why we have a milestone exercise, in which the user is presented with a text, usually a short story or a tongue twister, provided by the SLP, to be read aloud. This whole process is recorded and can be seen by the SLP. Additionally, the overall progress of users, including when and how often they complete exercises, is recorded and can be viewed by the SLP. This allows them to evaluate their patient's performance and ensure that any issues can be corrected during in-person sessions.
